Jurafsky and Martin (2023) offer a book-length treatment of Large Language Models (LLMs) and related topics. Douglas (2023) is a an excellent review. I flesh out the mathematics of LLMs. I do not attempt to discuss the yet not understood and misterious reason of why LLMs work so well, in spite of their relatively simple mathematical inner workings.

LLMs infer the probability distribution on the space of sequences of words and enable sampling of text from this distribution, hence they are are generative. The distribution of a sequence (corpus) of words/tokens1 \(w_{1:n} := (w_1,\ldots,w_n)\) is determined by the conditional distributions of the next word in the sequence given the previous words,

\[P(w_{1:n}) = \prod_{k=1}^n P(w_k|w_{1:(k-1)}). \tag{1}\]

If we learn these conditional distributions, we can sample from the distribution of the next word conditional on the sequence so far, append the predicted word to the existing sequence and continue the process to generate a text of desired length. This process is known as autoregressive generation or causal language modeling generation.

Greedy decoding chooses the most probable token (word) at each step, instead of randomly sampling from the predicted conditional distribution of the next word. Choices that look good now might turn later to be suboptimal, and in practice one employs beam search. We keep \(k\) possible tokens at each step (the beam width), thus we select the best \(k\) tokens. At the next step, each of these \(k\) choices is extended incrementally being passed to distinct decoders, and we keep the \(k\) most probable sequences of length two etc.

LLMs are trained by minimizing a measure of distance between the predicted and the actual next word for the entire corpus. This self-supervised capability of LLMs removes the need for labeled data and makes possible the use of the entire internet corpus of around \(10^{12}\) words for training (Douglas, 2023). It justifies the "large" terminology in LLMs and it allows autoregressive LLMss to excel at text generation, summarization, question answering and even at machine translation, exceeding the performance of supervised models trained specifically on pairs of sequences in two different languages.

N-gram models

The simplest language model is the N-gram model. It imposes the Markov condition that only the previous \(N-1\) words are useful in predicting the probability of the current word,

\[P(w_k|w_{1:(k-1)}) = P(w_k|w_{(k-N+1):(k-1)}). \tag{2}\]

In particular, the 1-gram model assumes that words are independent, \(P(w_{1:k}) = \prod_{i=1}^{k} P(w_i)\). The conditional probabilities are estimated from the corpus by the corresponding empirical distributions,2

\[P(w_k|w_{k-1},\ldots,w_{k-N+1}) =\frac{\text{Number of occurrences of } w_{(k-N+1):k}}{\text{Number of occurrences of } w_{(k-N+1):(k-1)}}.\tag{3}\]

The N-gram model with \(N=3\) or \(N=4\) works quite well. However, even for an \(N\) as low as 20, the entire internet corpus will contain only a tiny fraction of the possible (and sensible) twenty words strings, making the estimation of probabilities based on empirical distributions impractical.

Embeddings

To progress beyond such simple N-grams models, words \(w\) need to be converted to vectors \(e(w) \in \mathbb{R}^{p\times 1}\) using an embedding map \(e(\cdot)\). Let \(\mathcal{V}\) be the vocabulary and \(v:=|\mathcal{V}|\) the number of (unique) words. Any word \(W \in \mathcal{V}\) can be represented as a one-hot \(v\)-dimensional vector \(\mathbf{1}_w : V \to \{0,1\}\) with \(\mathbf{1}_w(w') =1\) if \(w=w'\) and \(0\) otherwise. Even better, we can use the \(N\)-gram co-occurrence vector in \(\mathbb{R}^{v \times 1}\) with coordinate \(i\) indicating the number of co-occurrences of words \(i,w\) in an N-gram. The \(v \times v\) matrix with columns given by these vectors is called the co-occurrence matrix. These sparse high dimensional vectors seem wasteful and they can be embedded in a space of dimension \(p<<n\) by replacing them by their scores relative to the first \(p\) principal components (or other more refined methods).

The current state of the art LLMs learn a \(p \times v\) embedding matrix \(E\) by treating the elements of \(E\) as additional parameters and convert a one-hot vector \(\mathbf{1}_w \in \mathbb{R}^{v \times 1}\) for word \(w\) into its embedding \(E \mathbf{1}_w \in \mathbb{R}^{p \times 1}\).

A blueprint for LLMs

The use of an embedding map \(e(\cdot)\) provides a general approach to build Markovian LLMs with a sliding window of over the last \(L\) words:

  • Given a sequence of \(L\) words \(w_{t-1},\ldots,w_{t-L}\), concatenate their embeddings into a vector \(x_{(t-L):(t-1)} \in \mathbb{R}^{(L \cdot p) \times 1}\).

  • Use a function \(F(\cdot;\theta):\mathbb{R}^{L \times p} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{p \times 1}\) parametrized by \(\theta\) to \(x\) to produce a prediction vector \(z_t=F(x_{(t-L):(t-1)};\theta) \in \mathbb{R}^{p \times 1}\).

  • Output the probability mass function3 \(y_{t} \in \Delta(\mathcal{V}) \subset \mathbb{R}^v\) of the next word \(w_t\) based on its (cosine) similarity to \(z\),

    \[y_t:=\text{softmax}(\beta \langle e(w'), z_t \rangle \; : \; w' \in \mathcal{V} ) \in \Delta(V). \tag{4}\]

In other words,

\[y_t(w) = P(w_t = w|w_{(t-L):(t-1)}) = \frac{e^{\beta \langle e(w), z_t \rangle}}{\sum_{w' \in \mathcal{V}} e^{\beta \langle e(w'), z \rangle } }. \tag{5}\]

The hyperparameter \(\beta\) is the inverse temperature parameter (inspired by statistical mechanics). A large \(\beta \rightarrow \infty\) places probability close to \(1\) on the word with the most similar embedding to the prediction \(z\). If the embedding function is linear, \(e(w):=E \mathbf{1}_w\) with \(E\) a \(p \times v\) matrix, then the output is simply

\[y_t = \text{softmax} (\beta \mathbf{1}'_w E' z_t \; : \; w \in \mathcal{V} ) = \text{softmax} (\beta I E' z_t) = \text{softmax} (\beta E' z_t), \tag{6}\]

where \(I\) is the \(v \times v\) identity matrix formed by stacking the row vectors \(\mathbf{1}'_w\) for \(w \in \mathcal{V}\).

The parameters \(\theta\) are estimated by minimizing the average cross-entropy loss at \(t\) of predicting next words. The cross entropy between the predicted probability distribution \(y_t\) over the vocabulary \(V\) and the "data" distribution which is a Dirac (atomic) distribution putting probability \(1\) on the observed word \(w_t\) is

\[L_{CE}(y_t,\mathbf{1}_{w_t}|w_{(t-L):(t-1)}) := - \langle \mathbf{1}_{w_t}, \log y_t \rangle. \tag{7}\]

In other words, we simply calculate the negative of the log probability predicted by the model for the actual observed word. The average cross-entropy loss over the entire corpus \(w_{1:n}\) is

\[ L := \frac 1n \sum_{t=1}^n L_{CE}(y_t,\mathbf{1}_{w_t}|w_{(t-L):(t-1)}) \tag{8}\]

We give the model the correct history to predict the next word (teacher forcing) rather than the predicted most probable words obtained previously. It is more common to report the perplexity \(\exp(L_{CE})\) instead of the cross-entropy loss \(L_{CE}\). It represents the inverse of the average probability of predicting correctly the next word. State of the art LLMs achieve perplexity of around 20, meaning that they predict correctly the next word occurring in training data about once in 20 tries.

The minimization with respect to the parameters \(\theta\) is done via stochastic gradient descent. We start with an initial guess for the parameters (usually independent normal with mean zero and variance \(1/p\)). For each batch of around \(10^6\) words, we update the parameters as

\[\theta := \theta - \eta \nabla_\theta L(\theta), \tag{9}\]

where \(L\) in equation (8) is restricted to the batch and \(\eta\) is the learning rate hyperparameter, around \(10^{-4}\).

Feedforward Neural Networks (FFNs)

FFNs are natural candidates to describe \(F\). With enough parameters, they can approximate arbitrarily well any function. FFNs with \(d\) hidden layers are compositions of affine functions \(W\) and a nonlinear activation function \(\sigma\) (applied component wise)

\[F_{FFN} := \sigma \circ W_d \circ W_{d-1} \circ \sigma \circ \ldots W_1 \circ \sigma \circ W_0. \tag{10}\]

FFNs can handle relatively large \(L\) of around \(100\). This approach never gained traction and the transformer architecture, accommodating larger \(L\), came to be dominant.

Recurrent Neural Network(s) (RNNs)

Before the advent of transformers, RNNs were favored over FFNs for sequence modeling as they can capture arbitrarily long dependence on the past (large \(L\)). The prediction ("state") of a RNN at each stage depends on the previous prediction, in addition to the previous words embeddings. This "recursive" property leads to a dynamical system and to long-term dependence ("memory"):

\[z_t = F_{RRN}(z_{t-1},x_{(t-L):(t-1)};\theta). \tag{11}\]

A typical joint parametrization of \(F\) and of the embedding map \(e(\cdot)\) with \(L=1\) consists of a \(p \times v\) embedding matrix \(E\) and scaling matrices \(U \in \mathbb{R}^{p \times p}\), \(W \in \mathbb{R}^{v \times p}\), \(V \in \mathbb{R}^{p \times v}\) and activation function \(g\):

\[e_t = E \mathbf{1}_{w_t}, \; z_t = g(U z_{t-1}+W e_t),\; y_t =\text{softmax}(V z_t). \tag{12}\]

It is common to take \(V =\beta E'\) to reduce the number of parameters. RNNs can be stacked, by using the entire sequence of outputs from one RNN as an input sequence to another one. Small changes make RNNs applicable to other tasks.4 For sequence labeling where the network needs to assign one of \(k\) classes to each word in a sequence (e.g., part-of-speech tagging), \(V\) is a \(k \times p\) matrix \(Z\) resulting in tag probabilities over the given set of \(k\) labels.

For sequence classification (sentiment analysis, spam detection etc), the last state \(z_n\) is used as a compressed representation of the entire sequence \(w_{1:n}\) and passed to a FFN that chooses a target class via a softmax. Bidirectional RNNs run an RNN on the sequence (left to right), and an independent RNN on the inverted sequence (right to left). The terminal states of the two networks, emphasizing information from both ends of the sequence, are concatenated and passed to the FFN.

RNNs are difficult to train with long sentences, as the repeated compositions of functions implicit in the recursive structure leads to gradients featuring repeated multiplications and possibly driven to zero (the vanishing gradients problem).5

RNNs cannot process words in a window of length \(L\) in parallel, hence computational time is \(L\). Transformers, discussed next, dispense with recursion and are amenable to parallel processing. They have become the dominant architecture.

Transformers

A transformer block with \(H\) heads consists of a composition of two layers, each mapping some sequence of embeddings \(x_{1:L} = (x_1,\ldots,x_L) \in \mathbb{R}^{(Lp)\times 1}\) into a sequence \(z_{1:L} = (z_1,\ldots,z_L) \in \mathbb{R}^{(Lp)\times 1}\) of equal length. The first layer is a FFN with a single hidden layer with \(p_h\) neurons applied component by component, \(z_i = F_{FFN}(x_i)\). The self-attention attention layer consists of \(H\) "heads" ran in parallel on the same input \(x_{1:L}\). Let \(q:=p / H\) the embedding dimension divided by the number of heads. The outputs inputs \(u_i \in \mathbb{R}^{q \times 1}\) with \(i \in \{1,\ldots,L\}\) of each head are a scaled weighted average of the previous inputs \(x_j\) with \(j \le i\), with weights determined by the (generalized) similarity between the current and previous words,

\[ u_i := W \sum_{j=1}^i \alpha_{ij} x_j,\; \alpha_{ij}:= \text{softmax} (x'_i B x_j \: : \: 1 \le j \le i). \tag{13}\]

The matrix \(B \in \mathbb{R}^{p \times p}\) emphasizes desired parts of the embeddings vectors when calculating similarities. When \(B\) is the identity matrix, we recover the usual cosine similarity. The scaling matrix \(W \in \mathbb{R}^{q \times p}\) shrinks the dimension of the each output \(u_i\) to \(q\). Finally, the outputs of the \(H\) heads run in parallel with different \(B\) and \(W\) parameters are concatenated to produce the desired output \(z_{1:L} \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times p}\). To focus on the essentials, I omitted two additional ingredients in a transformer block: a layer normalization step, and residual connections between the attention and the fully connected layer (Jurafsky and Martin, 2023).

Permuting \(x_{1:i}\) (changing the order of words) in (13) does not change \(u_i\). To avoid this invariance, word position is added explicitly by extending each input \(x_i\) to \((x_i,b_i) \in \mathbb{R}^{(p+L) \times 1}\), with \(b_i\) the \(i\)-th standard basis vector in \(\mathbb{R}^{L \times 1}\). We learn a combined representation \(z \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times p}\) defined by

\[ z_i =W_z \max \{W_x x_i + W_b b_i,0\} \in \mathbb{R}^p, \; W_z \in \mathbb{R}^{p \times m}, W_x \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times p}, W_b \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times L}.\tag{14}\]

The total computation required by the transformer scales as \(L^2\), making very large \(L\) impractical. As an illustration, the 175B parameters Chat GPT-3 uses an embedding dimension \(p = 12288\), a hidden layer dimension \(p_h = 4p\), a window length \(L = 4096\) or \(8192\), a number of heads \(H = 96\) and \(96\) stacked transformer blocks.
